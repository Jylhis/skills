# Thai tones and the tone-rule system

Thai has **five tones**. Tone is phonemic — it distinguishes otherwise-identical
words — so it must be learned with every syllable, not added later.

| Tone | Paiboon mark | Contour | Cue | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|-----|---------|
| Mid | a (no mark) | level, mid | → | นา *naa* (field) |
| Low | à | low, level-ish | ↓ | ข่าว *khàao* (news) |
| Falling | â | high→low | ↘ | ข้าว *khâao* (rice) |
| High | á | high, rising-ish | ↑ | ม้า *máa* (horse) |
| Rising | ǎ | low→high | ↗ | เขา *khǎo* (he/she) |

Drill these as **minimal pairs** so the learner hears the contrast, not just the
word. Pair each with its arrow cue (dual coding).

## How tone is determined (the rule the script encodes)

A syllable's tone is a function of four things:
1. **Initial consonant class** — mid, high, or low (every consonant belongs to one).
2. **Syllable type** — *live* (open long vowel, or sonorant ending ง น ม ย ว ญ ร ล)
   vs *dead* (stop ending /k/,/p/,/t/, or open short vowel).
3. **Tone mark**, if any — ่ *mai ek*, ้ *mai tho*, ๊ *mai tri*, ๋ *mai chattawa*.
4. **Vowel length** (only needed to split the low-class dead case).

### With a tone mark (reliable, highest priority)

| Mark | Mid/High class | Low class |
|------|----------------|-----------|
| ่ mai ek | low | falling |
| ้ mai tho | falling | high |
| ๊ mai tri | high | (rare) high |
| ๋ mai chattawa | rising | (rare) rising |

### Without a tone mark

| Class | Live | Dead (short vowel) | Dead (long vowel) |
|-------|------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Mid | mid | low | low |
| High | rising | low | low |
| Low | mid | high | falling |

This is why **consonant class matters**: high vs low class with no mark gives
rising vs mid (live) — completely different tones.

## Silent leading consonants (class changers)
- **ห นำ**: a silent ห before a low-class consonant raises the syllable to **high
  class** (หมา *mǎa* = rising, not mid). Likewise ห before ง ญ น ม ย ร ล ว.
- **อ นำ**: silent อ before ย gives **mid class** — only four words: อย่า อยู่ อย่าง
  อยาก.

## Teaching sequence
1. Hear and produce the five tones in isolation (with the arrow cues).
2. Minimal-pair discrimination (khǎo/khào/khâao…).
3. Consonant classes (mid/high/low) as a property of each letter.
4. Live vs dead syllables; then the no-mark rule table.
5. The four tone marks and their effect by class.

When you give a word, always state its tone by name and show the contour; tag the
card `tone-<name>`.
